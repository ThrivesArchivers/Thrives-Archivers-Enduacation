E1

Explain the roles of membrane lipids and proteins in maintaining cell function. (Concise but comprehensive)

Model Answer — Membrane lipids & proteins
  1. Phospholipid bilayer (amphipathic): forms the barrier separating intracellular and extracellular compartments, creating a hydrophobic core that restricts passage of polar/charged molecules.
  2. Cholesterol: intercalates between phospholipids to modulate fluidity and mechanical stability and reduce passive permeability.
  3. Glycolipids/Glycoproteins: provide recognition/adhesion sites on the extracellular leaflet and can be targets for pathogens or signalling molecules.
  4. Integral proteins: transmembrane proteins function as channels, carriers and pumps (active transport), and receptors that convert extracellular signals into intracellular responses.
  5. Peripheral proteins: attach to membrane surfaces, act as scaffolds or enzymes, and connect membranes to the cytoskeleton to maintain shape and organize signalling complexes.
  6. Combined role: Membrane lipids provide the physical environment; proteins provide specificity — together they enable selective transport, signal transduction, intercellular interaction and compartmentalized biochemical activities.
E2

Describe how defects in sphingolipid metabolism can affect the nervous system. Mention one disease example and the underlying idea.

Model Answer — Sphingolipids and nervous system disease
  1. Sphingolipid basics: Sphingolipids (derived from sphingosine + fatty acid → ceramide) include sphingomyelin and glycolipids (cerebrosides, gangliosides) that are abundant in neural membranes and myelin.
  2. Metabolic defects: Enzyme deficiencies that degrade sphingolipids cause accumulation of substrates in neurons/glia, disrupting membrane function and myelin integrity.
  3. Example — Krabbe disease: Deficiency of galactocerebrosidase leads to psychosine accumulation, demyelination and severe neurological symptoms (tremors, paralysis, early death).
  4. Example — Tay–Sachs: Defect in hexosaminidase A causes GM2 ganglioside accumulation, progressive neurodegeneration, seizures and blindness in infants.
  5. Key idea: Sphingolipid metabolism is essential for myelin and neuronal membrane homeostasis; its disruption leads to toxic storage, demyelination and neurodegenerative disease.
