Essay 1: Explain the bicarbonate buffering system and how the lungs and kidneys compensate for a metabolic acidosis.

Model answer (summary):

The bicarbonate buffer system is the main extracellular buffer and involves the equilibrium: CO₂ + H₂O ↔ H₂CO₃ ↔ H⁺ + HCO₃⁻. Carbonic anhydrase accelerates CO₂ hydration to carbonic acid. In metabolic acidosis (decreased HCO₃⁻), the lungs compensate by increasing ventilation (hyperventilation) to lower PaCO₂, shifting the equation left and raising pH. The kidneys compensate over hours to days by increasing H⁺ secretion and regenerating/retaining HCO₃⁻ (via ammoniagenesis and H⁺ excretion), restoring bicarbonate levels. Clinical compensation is partial; both systems work together to return pH toward normal while the primary cause is treated.

Essay 2: A patient with diarrhoea presents with metabolic acidosis. Describe the pathophysiology, likely lab findings (pH, HCO₃⁻, PaCO₂), and immediate management steps.

Model answer (summary):

Pathophysiology: Acute diarrhoea causes loss of bicarbonate-rich intestinal fluids, decreasing serum HCO₃⁻ and causing metabolic acidosis. Lab findings: low pH (<7.35), low HCO₃⁻ (often <22 mmol/L; severe cases <14), and compensatory low PaCO₂ from hyperventilation (e.g., PaCO₂ <40 mmHg). Immediate management: correct volume depletion with isotonic saline (to restore perfusion and renal bicarbonate reclamation), replace electrolytes (especially potassium), treat underlying cause (antimicrobials if infectious), and reserve IV bicarbonate only for severe acidosis (e.g., pH <7.0 or hemodynamic compromise), being aware of risks (CO₂ rise, cerebral acidosis, hypokalemia).
