Essay Questions (5)

Below are 5 essay questions. Each has an expected answer/mark scheme so learners know what to include to earn full marks.

  1. Essay 1: Explain the difference between "disease" and "illness" and discuss why understanding this distinction is important for healthcare providers.
    Expected answer (mark scheme)

    Key points to include (full marks ~ 8–10 marks):

    • Define disease as objective, measurable pathology (diagnosed/tested). (2 marks)
    • Define illness as the subjective experience of symptoms and suffering reported by the person. (2 marks)
    • Explain the importance: clinicians must recognise that patients may feel unwell despite normal tests (illness without disease) and may have silent disease without feeling ill. (2 marks)
    • Provide examples (e.g., depression may be felt but not always test-positive; hypertension may be asymptomatic). (2 marks)
  2. Essay 2: Compare and contrast the biomedical (Western) model and the holistic (WHO) model of health. What are strengths and limitations of each?
    Expected answer (mark scheme)

    Key points (full marks ~ 10–12 marks):

    • Describe biomedical model: focuses on absence of disease, body-as-machine, curative interventions, measurable data (2–3 marks).
    • Describe holistic/WHO model: complete physical, mental and social wellbeing; positive definition; acknowledges social causes (2–3 marks).
    • Strengths of biomedical: precise diagnostics, effective acute treatments, measurable outcomes (2 marks).
    • Limitations of biomedical: ignores social determinants, mental and social aspects, preventive care (2 marks).
    • Strengths of holistic: addresses mental/social wellbeing, prevention, public health; more person-centred (2 marks).
    • Limitations of holistic: harder to measure, may be broad/vague for policy unless operationalised (2 marks).
  3. Essay 3: Describe the salutogenesis model and give two practical examples of salutogenic interventions in public health programmes.
    Expected answer (mark scheme)

    Key points (full marks ~ 8 marks):

    • Definition: salutogenesis (Antonovsky) focuses on origins of health, resources, coping, and what keeps people well rather than what causes disease (2–3 marks).
    • Explain concept of sense of coherence / resources like social support, resilience (2 marks).
    • Give two examples: e.g., in HIV programmes — ART adherence support + peer groups; community gardens and training to improve food security and mental wellbeing. Explain how each builds resources. (3 marks)
  4. Essay 4: Using Dahlgren & Whitehead's model, explain how both individual lifestyle and broad socioeconomic factors interact to affect health. Provide a local example.
    Expected answer (mark scheme)

    Key points (full marks ~ 10 marks):

    • Briefly outline the layered model (individual, social/community networks, living/working conditions, general socioeconomic/environmental conditions). (2 marks)
    • Explain interaction: e.g., poverty reduces access to healthy food (environment), which influences lifestyle (diet), raising chronic disease risk; living conditions affect stress and mental health. (3–4 marks)
    • Give a local example: e.g., in a low-income neighbourhood, poor sanitation and overcrowding increase RTIs while low income limits nutritious food leading to malnutrition and poor school performance — connect layers. (3–4 marks)
  5. Essay 5: Discuss the relationship between health and development. How can improving health contribute to national development, and how can development also improve health?
    Expected answer (mark scheme)

    Key points (full marks ~ 10 marks):

    • Explain health as a driver of development: healthier populations are more productive, reduced healthcare costs, better education outcomes. Give example like smallpox eradication reducing disability and boosting productivity. (4 marks)
    • Explain development as determinant of health: infrastructure, sanitation, education and economic growth lead to better health services and outcomes. (3 marks)
    • Discuss shared goals (SDGs) and give a short policy implication (2–3 marks).
